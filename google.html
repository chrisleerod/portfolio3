Introduction

I applied to Google‚Äôs 2020 UX summer internship and was instructed to choose between two exercises to design a solution for. I was given a seven-day deadline to research, wireframe, and design my product.

The challenge

‚ÄúYour school wants to strengthen the community by encouraging experienced students to connect with new students and help them adjust to campus life. Design an experience that allows mentors and mentees to discover each other. Consider the needs of both mentors and mentees, including how someone may become a mentor and how to connect mentors to mentees.‚Äù

Defining the sprint

Given the short deadline, I decided to use Google Ventures' Design sprint methodology as a very general guideline. this helped me set goals and manage my time.

01

Understand

As an important, foundational step, I asked some critical questions to understand the problem thoroughly.

How might we allow students to connect and discover other students and mentors, become mentors, and satisfy the needs of both mentors and mentees with MentorU?

How do current first-year students seek guidance? How do they connect with other students? How do they discover new opportunities?

After forming a couple preliminary questions, I interviewed students and conducted a survey to collect both quantitative and qualitative data. This allowed me to generate user insights, personas, and better empathize with fellow students.

02

Define

This phase utlized the research from the previous phase in order to better understand the audience and create user insights to form personas. This would allow me to better cover the evident issues and focus on what is most important.

There is an evident problem

The average student feels as though gaining useful advice, and mentorship is something that should be easier.

Not much has changed

Speaking to both first-year and non-first-year students confirmed that 3+ years difference hadn‚Äôt changed the process much. The problem is something that most students experience.

There is interest in volunteering

The responses from the non-first-year students give a promising outlook for having enough mentors to guide first-year and transfer students.

Thinking of integrations

After generating some user insights, I thought about ways that could assist creating a solution for the problem. I wanted to create an application that was a place of familiarity. My university is apart of the G Suite system, so integrating things such as Google Calendar was important.

Using personas to identify pain points

Once there was a clear hierarchy of the problems at hand, I began creating personas to give myself a clearer view of who I was designing this application for. I wanted to keep in mind that there were students at different levels of experience, and some new students aren‚Äôt freshman. They can also be transfer students. So moving forward, I created three personas: The Freshman, The Transfer Student, and The Experienced Senior.

First-Year Student

Emir Aldred

Emir is a first-semester student excited about college but doesn‚Äôt know where to start. He likes the idea of joining a club but is a little timid, nor does he know where he can find someone who he can talk to about this.

Second-Year Transfer Student

Angela Donaldson

Angela is a second-year transfer student who has the ropes of college, but could use some help navigating the campus and getting to know the event system a little better.

Fourth-Year Student

Andrew Wu

Andrew is a high-achieving fourth-year student who loves volunteering in the community. He wants to add a couple things to his resume that demonstrates his leadership qualities, but doesn‚Äôt really know where he could start.

Considering the experienced students

One group of students that would help the process of helping new students would be experienced students. How would they become mentors? I wanted to create a process that offered both reward and opportunity for good students; however, I did not want to make it exclusive. I considered a GPA threshold of 3.0 would grant experienced students access to become a mentor automatically. If a student did not meet these requirements, he or she could apply on campus and be interviewed.

03-04

Sketch/Decide

This phase utlized the research from the previous phase in order to better understand the audience and create user insights. This would allow me to better cover the evident issues and focus on what is most important.

Further iterations

After analyzing the initial sketches, I began to draw some questions to either validate or invalidate my design decisions. This would allow me to refine and decide what worked and didn‚Äôt work. This phase allowed me to reveal some holes in my designs that I needed to address. Some of these holes included notifications and ways to review and respond to connection requests. I also used this time to reevaluate the navigation system I had in place.

Validating the iterations

Not only did these iterations allow me to create more refined, user-experience-oriented designs, but it allowed me to get a grasp of what the overall direction and mission of this application was going to be. What screens could I consolidate into one? Which interfaces could be adjusted to remove unnecessary information? I was able to view the experience as a map instead of a linear timeline. My ideation was dramatically shifted thanks to these small roadblocks and changes.

05

Prototype

After finalizing the wireframe and sketches, I then moved onto low-fidelity prototyping while using Material UI as a framework. This expedited the design process which allowed for more thoughtful decisions. I created a user flow diagram prior to completing the prototype via InVision Studio.

Conducting user testing

After designing and creating a functional prototype in InvisionApp, I conducted some user tests with three users. No context of the design was given prior to testing. The goal was to hear from unbiased users and how they felt about the functionality and experience as a whole.

What did user testing accomplish?

Getting user opinions and testing data proved to show some weaknesses in the initial designs. I went back and reiterated the designs in order strengthen the user's experience. For example, a clear problem was the navigation to view both potential and current connections. The initial designs I came up with did not allow the user to easily navigate between the two options.

The final designs

Wrapping up the sprint, I created a high-fidelity prototype featuring in-depth animations to present to Google.

Reflection

Overall, I had an absolutely amazing time working on this project. I learned a ton about the Google Venture design process and it also allowed me to think critically to fit my design solutions in just a six-day period. Overall, I would have liked to validate my designs further with more user testing with the high-fidelity prototypes, but was not able to due to time constraints.

The outcome

I passed the design challenge and was moved to project matching. Unfortunately, the Google UX internship for Summer 2020 was canceled due to COVID-19.
